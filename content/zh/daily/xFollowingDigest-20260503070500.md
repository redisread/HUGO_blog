Your armpit is a bioreactor producing a thiol detectable at parts per trillion. Your nose is the only one in the room not picking it up.

The smell coming off your armpits is mostly 3-methyl-3-sulfanylhexan-1-ol, a thiol that Staphylococcus hominis produces by breaking down a precursor in your apocrine sweat. Human noses detect this class of compound at picograms per liter of air. A few molecules per breath is enough to register.

But your own nose adapts to you specifically. Olfactory receptors are GPCRs, and continuous exposure phosphorylates them. Calcium floods the cilia. Adenylyl cyclase activity drops. The cyclic nucleotide-gated channels that fire the neuron lose their affinity for cAMP. The whole cascade collapses in seconds to minutes. Your cortex layers a second filter on top, tagging your scent signature as "self" so the brain stops processing it.

When you can faintly smell yourself, your receptors are running at maybe 1% of their actual sensitivity to that compound. The person walking past you has full receptor density, fully sensitized, firing on a molecule they've never been exposed to before.

You're listening through earplugs. They're standing next to the speaker.

This is also why you can't smell your own house, your own car, or perfume on yourself ten minutes after you spray it. Recovery requires about 20 minutes of complete absence from the source. Anything less and you're still adapted.

You're catching the last 1%. They've been at 100% for an hour.
